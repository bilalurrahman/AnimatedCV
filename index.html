chapter one

The Traveler.

Every wonder world needs someone who reads the map — and someone who chases the snowflakes. Bilal does both.

For a decade, Bilal built the systems that banks and bureaus actually run on — Clean Architecture microservices, CQRS pipelines, batch engines and messaging backbones on .NET and SQL Server, fronted by Angular. That trail taught him the hard part of enterprise software isn't the code; it's making correctness, auditability and compliance the default path.

Now he applies that lesson to AI. At SIMAH, the Saudi Credit Bureau, he owns the platform layer that lets AI coding assistants work on regulated codebases: a governed LLM gateway with data-residency routing, coding standards encoded as machine-checkable rules, and autonomous agent loops that review, verify and shepherd pull requests before a human ever looks at them.

He's also a doctoral researcher studying the same question academically: can governance be embedded into AI coding assistants so regulated institutions get the speed without the risk? His bet — and his daily work — says yes.
